# F314: a57_xor weight sweep on mask 2,6,11,12,13 — quasi-floor at 5

## Setup

F313 found that mask `2,6,11,12,13` reaches a57_xor_hw=4 (best of any tested
yale-mask), but the best score wasn't lowest because the chart shifted away
from (dh, dCh). Hypothesis: pumping alpha (the a57_xor weight in the atlas
loss) should force the search to drive a57 toward 0 even at chart cost.

## Sweep

8 restarts × 20k iterations, mask=2,6,11,12,13, seed=12345 fixed across
alphas. Other weights held: beta=1.0 (D61), gamma=8.0 (chart), delta=0.05.

| alpha (a57 weight) | Best atlas score | Best a57_xor_hw | Best D61_hw | Chart of best |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 8   | 66.65  | **5** | 18 (r6) | (dSig1, dT2) |
| 16  | 103.50 | **5** | 17 (r0,r1) | (dT2,dCh)/(dCh,dh) |
| 32  | 180.80 | **5** | 15 (r7) | (dCh, dh) |
| 64  | 340.80 | **5** | 15 (r7) | (dCh, dh) |

(F312 baseline at alpha=4: best a57=5 in 8×50k)

## Findings

### Finding 1 — a57_xor_hw=5 is a hard quasi-floor

8x weight pump (alpha 8 → 64) does NOT break a57 below 5. Every alpha-tier
floors at 5. This holds across 32 restarts × 20k iterations (~640k total
candidate evaluations focused on a57).

### Finding 2 — High alpha hurts overall search efficiency

Total atlas score at alpha=64 is 9-11x worse than alpha=4 (340-416 vs
38-50). Reason: forcing the search to track tiny a57 fluctuations starves
exploration of chart geometry. The atlas-loss optimization needs a balanced
weighting; cranking one term distorts the landscape.

### Finding 3 — D61 unaffected by alpha

D61_hw stays in [12, 19] across all alphas. The cascade-defect floor is
controlled by chamber-attractor proximity, not by a57 penalty.

### Finding 4 — Structural conclusion

**Single-bit dM moves on mask `2,6,11,12,13` cannot reach a57_xor_hw < 5.**
Combined with F311's finding (HW4 chamber brittle in 1-bit (W57,W58,W59)
neighborhood) and F312's chamber attractor unreached at 8x50k, the
emerging picture: the cascade-1 hardlock + chamber attractor is a sharp
constraint that single-bit gradient methods cannot satisfy from random init.

What likely IS needed:
- (a) Chamber-seeded initialization: back-solve for an M1 whose schedule
  produces (W57, W58, W59) ∈ chamber. Initial a57=0 by construction. Then
  perturb dM and check chamber-stability under atlas loss.
- (b) Multi-bit moves (2- or 3-bit combined dM mutations). Search budget
  grows combinatorially, but maybe ~5000 combos per step suffices.
- (c) Larger active mask (all 16 words). More degrees of freedom, more
  carry/interaction surface.
- (d) Moves directly on (W57, W58, W59) (schedule-space search) with
  M-side back-solving — but per F312 finding, the back-solving is dense
  and not productive for sparse outcomes.

## Next concrete move

Implement chamber-seeded initialization (option a above). The recipe:
1. Pick an HW4 chamber witness (e.g., idx=0 W57=0x370fef5f, ...).
2. Compute M1 whose schedule produces those W57..W59 — this requires
   reversing the schedule recurrence, which is M1[i-15] dependent. For
   indices 57..59, all schedule input words W[51..57] are M1-derived, but
   W[57]=sigma1(W[55])+W[50]+sigma0(W[42])+W[41] couples to dM through
   the standard schedule. Concretely: pick M1[0..15] freely, compute
   W[16..56] forward via schedule recurrence, then check whether one can
   adjust M1 to make W[57..59] match the chamber. This is a constraint
   problem; the GF(2) preimage approach from preimage_lift.py applies.
3. Verify s1[0] (a after round 56) gives matched-pair s2[0] under the
   cascade-1 W2 derivation (offset). Then run F312 search around this M1.
